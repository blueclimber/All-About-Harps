Introduction

The harp is a well known musical instrument. It is often assiciated with angels, or the music indicating a dream sequence in a video. But there is a lot to learn about these beautiful instruments. Together we can explore many facets of the harp, including the parts of the harp, basic techniques, and history, as well as some of the types of harps and a sampling of harp music.

The harp is a stringed musical instrument that has individual strings running at an angle to its soundboard; the strings are plucked with the fingers. Harps can be made and played in various ways, standing or sitting, and in orchestras or concerts. Its most common form is triangular in shape and made of wood. Some have multiple rows of strings and pedal attachments. Ancient depictions of harps were recorded in Mesopotamia (now Iraq), Persia (now Iran) and Egypt, and later in India and China. By medieval times harps had spread across Europe. Harps were found across the Americas where it was a popular folk tradition in some areas. Distinct designs also emerged from the African continent. Harps have symbolic political traditions and are often used in logos, including in Ireland. Historically, strings were made of sinew (animal tendons). Other materials have included gut (animal intestines), plant fiber, braided hemp, cotton cord, silk, nylon, and wire. wikipedia.org/wiki/Harp

Techniques

Harps are usually played by pulling the harp back onto it's back feet, resting the neck of the harp on the right shoulder of the harpist, and plucking the strings with the tips of the fingers. Usually the right hand plays the higher notes, and left hand plays the lower notes.

There are many techniques and methods of playing that are used, here are a few examples.

  • Glissando

    this is when the strings are quickly played in a upward or downward motion

  • Flat octaves or thumbs

    Usually in the bass, the harpist plays moving octaves or single notes, quickly stopping the sound after each note to mimic the sound of an upright bass.

  • PDLT

    the haprist plays low on the strings near the soundbaord to mimic the sound of a guitar

  • Tapping the sound board

    the harpist can use the soundboard as a drum, tapping on it with the tips of the fingers

  • Fingernail strums

    using all of the fingers of one or both hands the harpist can strum quickly downward with the backside of their fingernails to make an exciting percussive sound.

  • Fingernail glissando

    using the backside of the fingernails for downward glissandi, and the backside of the thumbnail for upward glissandi, this makes a sharper sounding glissando.

History

Harps have been known since antiquity in Asia, Africa, and Europe, dating back at least as early as 3000 BCE. The instrument had great popularity in Europe during the Middle Ages and Renaissance, where it evolved into a wide range of variants with new technologies, and was disseminated to Europe's colonies, finding particular popularity in Latin America. wikipedia.org/wiki/Harp

According to Wikipedia, the earliest harps were found in Sumer in 3500 BCE, with the earliest visual depictions of a harp being found in Egypt dated as early as 3000 BCE.

Basic Anatomy of the Harp

  • Feet

    The harp rests on 4 feet and is pulled back onto the back 2 when being played

  • Base

    The base of pedal haro contains the mechanisms for the pedals

  • Soundboard

    The soundboard vibrates, creating the full sound. On average, the larger the soundboard, the bigger and richer the sound.

  • Soundbox

    Sometimes called the body, the soundbox is hollow, allowing for amplification of the sound

  • Pillar

    Often referred to as the column, this is the front of the harp. In a pedal harp, rods will run up the pillar to mechanisims in the neck of the harp.

  • Neck and Shoulder

    Not pointed out in the image, the neck is the curving top of the harp. The shoulder generally rests on the shoulder of the harpist when being played.

  • Crown

    The tallest point on the harp

  • Strings

    Often made of sheep gut, nylon, or wound metal, the strings are plucked to create the sound.

  • Tuning pins

    The strings are wound through the tuning pins. These can then be turned to tune the harp. Harpists get good at tuning their harps, because it needs to be done often.

  • Pedal

    Pedals are operated by the feet, and allow for the harpist to change the sound of specific strings.

  • Tuning disks

    The pedals connect through the base, pillar and neck of the harp to mechanisms that turn the tuning disks. This allows each string to have three lengths - flat, natural, and sharp.

Folk Harp

Often referred to as celtic harps or lever harps, the folk harp is the classic image that one thinks of when they imagine a harp. They are on the smaller side, sometimes small enough to fit on the lap, and sometimes larger to sit on a box or the floor. They may be fitted with sharping levers, which allow the harpist the alter the tuning of a string by a half step.

Welsh Triple Strung Harp

A lesser known harp outside of Wales is the Welsh Triple Strung Harp. These harps have three rows of strings running parralel to each other. The outer two rows are tuned to be the natural notes of the scale (like the white keys on the piano). The inner row is tuned to be the shraps and flats (like the plack keys on the piano). This image is from an article by the Metropolitan Museum of Art

Unlike many other types of harps, the Welsh Triple Strung is traditionally pulled back on the left shoulder and the higher notes are generally played with the left hand and the lower notes are generally played with the right hand. Because of this the strings are strung on the right side of the harp instead of the left.

Pedal Harp

The large harp that is usually seen in an orchestra or concert setting is a pedal harp. These come in a few sizes, The Concert Grand which is the largest, the Semi Grand which is usually only one string smaller and an inch or so shorter than the Concert Grand, and various smaller student sizes.

Pedal harps are vastly more complicated than their levered counterparts. Instead of having an additional row of strings or sharping levers to get the additional notes of the scale, pedal harps are fitted with seven pedals at the back of the base of the harp. These pedals are connected to rods that go through the column of the harp, up to a series of mechanisms in the something. Those macanisms then control sharping pins at the top of each string. Each string has two sharping pins. When they are both open the string is flat, when one is open and the other is closed the string is natural, and when both are closed the string is sharp. Each pedal is associated with a specific note - there is one pedal that controls all of the C strings, one for each D and so on. Therefore, when the C pedal is in the sharp position, all of the C strings will sound a C# when played.

Because of the way pedal haprs are built, they accomodate more complex music, both due to their size and the ability to change a strings sound using pedals with the feet instead of changing a lever with the hand. This is why it is almost exclusively pedal harps that are seen within orchestras.

Electric Harps

Over recent years there has been the development of electric harps. One of the leading jazz harpists, Deborah Henson Conant has been largely responsible for this development as she wanted a harp that she could carry around on stage and plug in to an amp.
